01 · what it is

Supervise and govern
your agents

A live Kanban board with a human approval gate over any operation with effects. Observability and control, without trusting the model to behave.

It's not an agent: it's the layer that watches and governs agents while they work. LLM-agnostic and domain-agnostic.

02 · the problem

Fleets of agents are powerful. And opaque. And dangerous.

Dynamic workflows spawn dozens of agents in parallel. You can't see what they touch, and one irreversible action —delete, deploy, rotate keys— can take down production.

Asking the model in the prompt to "be careful" is a convention, not a control: it can skip it, get it wrong, or be manipulated by prompt injection.

03 · the approach

Control where the agent acts, not where it thinks.

Control doesn't live in the agent's brain (the LLM, unpredictable), but in the tool that every action with effects passes through. If the gate lives inside the tool, the effect is unreachable without crossing it — no matter which model invokes it.

Put the control at the toll on the only road to dangerous operations. Whoever wants to act, crosses the toll.

04 · the pillars

What makes it up

Agent board

Each agent is a card moving across columns. You see live what it's doing, its model, tokens and cost €.

Two-key approval

Operations with effects stop at Needs Input and wait for your Approve/Deny. Approving requires an operator token.

Deterministic gate

The decision is made by rules in code (policy.json): allow / ask / deny per tool. Reproducible and audited.

Multi-model by role

A different LLM per role; the verifier from another family kills self-approval bias.

06 · multi-provider

Claude, Gemini, OpenAI and local models

The same board supervises agents from any provider. A providers.py layer dispatches by model name and reports real tokens and cost per agent.

Not all tokens cost the same: budgets in € that distinguish local (~free) from foundational (expensive). If an expensive model overruns, it lands in "⚡ Out of budget".

07 · local vs foundational

Every token run locally is capital you keep

A key token-capital metric: how much of the work runs on local models (that you own, ~free) versus foundational ones (paid API). The more local, the more autonomy and the less variable cost.

local 72%foundational 28%

Growing autonomy

From 40% to 72% of work running local in 14 weeks. Less provider dependence.

Variable cost trending down

Every point that moves to local is API cost you stop paying.

The moat isn't just the loop: it's that more and more intelligence runs on models you own. Token capital rises; marginal cost falls.

08 · hardening (Phase 0)

Serious governance, zero dependencies

  • Approval binding to the payload hash — closes replay and TOCTOU.
  • Aggregate quotas per window — close threshold-chopping, by count or in €.
  • Verifiable hash-chain audit (audit.py verify) — tamper-evident.
  • Canonical summary composed by the server — the payload can't fool the operator.
  • CI lint — every tool with effects is decorated.
09 · two views

Operations today, capital over time

The same telemetry, two lenses. The operational board controls the loop minute by minute. The capital board reads that sediment —audit, cost, approvals— as the asset that accumulates: captured human judgment, data for evals, your moat.

Token Capital concept: the moat isn't the model you rent, it's the closed loop no one else can copy. Every approval is a label; every audit entry, an eval datapoint.
